Added a hover text color change for my navbar. This will help to increase the visual and functional continuity of my sight as the changes were made.

I didn't make any other changes to my navbar because from what I read this week I feel like it conforms to design standards. It's in a clear and easy place to reach. There isn't any ambiguity about where each link will take you. See this page for more information.

Navigation isn’t just a feature of a web site, it is the web site, in the same way that the building, the shelves, and the cash registers are Sears. Without it, there’s no there there. -Steve Krug

Overall Design

I didn't make any actual changes to my site. Namely because from the reading this week I guess I'm not really seeing anything that is wrong with what they described in their pages. This page talks about how it's important for a site to be clear, orderly, and seems trustworthy. A user should seem confident in a page when they first view it and I don't have any reason from either myself, or those I have shown my site to, to believe that there is anything generally wrong with the layout.

The primary purpose of graphic design are to:

  • Create a clear and visual hierarchy of constrast, so you can see at a glance what is important and what is peripheral.
  • Define functional regions of the page.
  • Group page elements that are related, so that you can see the structure in the content.

Final Thoughts

For this particular assignment this week I kind of struggled in understand exactly what was being asked of me. It wasn't necessarily that I didn't understand the assignment, because I read it through several times. It was more rather that the web page we were required to read this week was extremely verbose and it was hard to take everything that you were supposed to take out of it.

If I can be critical of their site and analyze it a bit, what they really need is a synopsis. This because you don't want to read through every single page every time you have a question about a topic. It is pretty difficult to navigate their site for that reason. You have a question about how graphics should contribute and you're faced with a wall of text and a creepy painting of a crab. The following pages on what graphics to use are really distracting because all of a sudden you have a page long image about whales. Your train of thought is disrupted and you're trying to understand what they have to say about whales, not what constitutes good images that add a new level of understanding for your readers. At least that is what happened to me.

So for some closing thoughts, I did my best to understand what I was supposed to take out of this week, but I'm pretty lost to be perfectly honest. There was a lot of material to take in and very little context to put it behind. With little context, a lot of that material being brought in is sent to your brain's temporary memory and isn't stored like it should be. From what I got is that your website should be easy to navigate, clearly define what it's purpose is, and readily present information and/or services to the user. Images should add to what's being portrayed, not distract, and not to just decorate (unless it's related).
